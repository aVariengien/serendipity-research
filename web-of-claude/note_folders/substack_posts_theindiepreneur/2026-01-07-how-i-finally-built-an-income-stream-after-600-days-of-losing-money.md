*The framework you can use too*

For 600 days straight, I wrote code, shipped, and killed products a week after publishing them.

It’s called ‘Ship fast’.

In short, it means that you should ship products as fast as you can and kill them if they don’t get any traction.

Now, it works. But for big creators with an audience.

I was convinced that it’d work for me as well. So I shipped a product every month or so, put some on ProductHunt and hoped for the best.

**Eighteen months.  
$0 revenue.  
Not even a single signal that I was getting better.**

I felt awful.

How I felt after 18 months with nothing to show for

And then came the moment everything snapped.

One day I decided that I am going to change something or I will keep failing forever. So I said to myself:

**“This is it. My last hoorah. Six months. One product. No escapes.”**

No more jumping to the next idea.  
No more quitting at the first sign of boredom or *“no traction”*.  
No more pretending I was learning when I was literally wasting time.

**Either I build something someone pays for, or I walk away from the dream entirely.**

That’s how WriteStack was born.

## The Illusion of Momentum

For a long time, I believed the same lie most engineers believe:

*“If I build a good enough solution, people will pay for it”.*

Wrong.

Each time I killed a project after a week of no traction, I missed a golden opportunity to learn:

* What users actually needed
* What to fix next
* Why did no one care
* How to **market** anything
* How to iterate.

In the past, entrepreneurs failed because they built something that took them months to complete and fell in love with their solution.

Nowadays, entrepreneurs fail because they lack the **patience** to build something, stick to it for long enough and pivot with user feedback.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!klHM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F969a027a-d3ca-42c4-8804-fc31c49fdfbd_480x400.gif)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!klHM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F969a027a-d3ca-42c4-8804-fc31c49fdfbd_480x400.gif)

It became so freaking easy to build and write anything, that we want results *now*. *Fast.*

Instead, we need to slow down, commit and focus.

## The Skill I Avoided for 600 Days

There’s one thing that I avoided doing for over 600 days.

**Talking to users.**

Why? because I thought my solution speaks for itself.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rUzb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F88a31a05-f52d-4eaf-b70c-2dd9233ac22d_368x254.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rUzb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F88a31a05-f52d-4eaf-b70c-2dd9233ac22d_368x254.png)

Hans Niemann after winning world champion Magnus Carlsen in chess

I thought, “If the product is good enough, people will get it.”

Spoiler: they didn’t.

Users don’t magically understand what you built. They don’t know why it matters. And they definitely won’t tell their friends about it if you never ask them what’s wrong.

**Users don’t care about you or your solution is.**

And it’s your job to make them care.

Once I committed to six months on one product, I didn’t have excuses anymore. I couldn’t rely on “the next idea” that will pop into my head and change everything.

I had to change my mindset from ‘1 week project’ to a ‘6 months’ one.

I needed to find ways to fill the time after building the MVP. I couldn’t just sit and stare at nothing.

So I started reaching out.  
Sending DMs.  
Showing demos.  
Asking questions.  
Offering things for free.  
Watching users use the product, learn and improve.

I did everything I didn’t do in the first 18 months of my journey. And let me tell you something (**honest**):

I learned more in 2 months than I learned in the first 18\.

And it all traced back to one decision:

**Stop resetting the game every week. Stay in the fight long enough to get better.**

There is no “fast” path to get profitable with your idea. In fact, I’ll go on a limb and tell you that your initial idea will probably suck.

## What Engineers Get Wrong About Side Income

Most engineers think a second income stream is just:

1. Build a clever solution
2. Launch
3. Get users
4. ???
5. Profit

Nope. Here’s the truth:

**1\. Brilliant code is useless without distribution.**  
If no one knows you exist, you don’t exist. Or like Grant Cardone says:

> *“If they don’t know you, they won’t flow you.”*

**2\. Your first idea will be wrong. Don’t treat it like a final product.**  
Treat it like a draft.

**3\.\*\*\*\*\* You must iterate with feedback. \*\*\*\*\*\***  
This is so freaking important. If you don’t talk to people, you might as well close shop and move on.

**4\. Pivoting is not a sign of a bad idea.**It’s a sign that you got a golden idea from feedback. Act on it.

**5\. You need patience more than you need talent.**  
Every engineer has talent. You need something else to win.

## **The Mindset Flip That Changed Everything**

When I finally committed to six months on one product, something in my head snapped into place.

I stopped acting like a casual ‘ship fast’ indie hacker. I started acting like a founder.

It always felt cringe to think of myself as a founder.

But after spending thousands of hours on one product, building it, refining it, talking to users, answering support DMs 24/7, I finally *feel* like one.

Here’s the mindset shift I had:

### **From “Ship Fast” → “Stay Long Enough to Learn”**

Shipping fast teaches you nothing if you kill the product before anyone can give you feedback.  
Commitment **forces** learning.

### **From “My idea is good” → “My idea is a draft”**

The moment you assume your idea is wrong, you suddenly become open to user feedback, pivots, and improvements.

### **From “I built it, so they’ll come” → “I built version 0, now the actual work begins”**

The MVP isn’t the time to rest and let the market do the work. It’s just an opportunity to start the work.

---

*For my gamers out there who played Wow, here’s the analogy:*

*It’s like reaching the level in which you can start raiding. It takes time, but you just start playing at 80\.*

### **From “I need traction in a week” → “I need clarity every week”**

* What did I learn?
* What did I improve?
* What did a user tell me?
* What changed in the product?

### **From “I’ll do it alone” → “Users are my roadmap”**

Users will show me the way.
