2 days later I was jobless, without any plan in sight.

Last year I shared all the products that I built, the platforms I was on and the money I made.

And there were many. But not a single dollar.

This newsletter is going to be raw. I’ll write it once and edit once.

## Short Summary of The First Year

During my first year I lived by the phrase:

> *“Ship it and they will come”*

Literally.

All I did was build products that I thought were solving a real problem, complete them to perfection and then publish on LinkedIn.

The results? $0, \<30 total non\-paying users.

I believed that it’s part of the journey. You just need to ship as many products as you can, as fast as you can, until something hits.

What a waste of time.

I built over 10 SaaS products, 1 Unity game, 1 mobile app. Wrote daily on LinkedIn/X.

Well, almost daily.

I repeated the same mistake with everything that I did. A mistake I talked about a lot.

### I built in the dark

No direction. No feedback. No idea what I am doing right and what I did wrong.

Only code writing and cringe posts on LinkedIn.

And it’s not that I didn’t have a small community to talk to and ask for feedback.

I just didn’t think it’s necessary.

I figured, if they don’t use it right off the bat, it’s probably not worth it and the idea is bad.

A mistake which I covered in details in the following post:

## This Is The Last Chance

After spending so much time and money, I decided I’ve had enough.

I will now build the product for 6 months. If it doesn’t work, I’m done and I am going to look for a job, work on my dream in the weekends.

This mindset alone changed everything.

From having a short timeframe for each product, 2\-4 weeks, I now need to build something and stick to it for 6 months.

No more ship fast mentality.

I now need to build and figure out how to move forward with the same product for 6 months.

I am repeating it because at that time, I had no idea how I would do it.

## WriteStack Story

So I started building WriteStack (Started as ArticleGenerate, Changed to WriteRoom, and now it’s WriteStack).

It started as a personalized Substack article outliner.

I believed, like with all my previous ideas, that this one is going to be a killer idea.

It’s a feature everybody needs and it solves a real problem.

Took me almost a month to build something extremely sophisticated, that learns exactly how and what the user writes about and generates outlines and ideas for articles based on that.

Since I had 5 months left to work with it, I figured, why not get some feedback?

**Spoiler alert**: the idea was terrible.

Nobody had a problem with writing their articles. They just didn’t have enough time to do it.

So the idea was useless. But it taught me something else.

People struggled a lot with the Notes feature of Substack. Specifically writing daily Notes and staying consistent.

Bingo! That’s what I should focus on.

So I pivoted, from generating personalized article outlines to generating personalized Notes and schedule them.

With more effort and more DMs sent, I onboarded [Tim Denning](https://open.substack.com/users/33842544-tim-denning?utm_source=mentions) onto WriteStack, and even got a testimonial from him (!!).

I realized I am onto something big here.

On April 6th, I got my first ever payment from [Kacper Wojaczek](https://open.substack.com/users/238671305-kacper-wojaczek?utm_source=mentions), as my first every paying user (thank you for the trust \<3\).

## Life After The First Paying User

I have finally cracked the code!

How to build a SaaS product and make it profitable. After hundreds of days of seeing no progress, something clicked.

I knew I am in the right direction, now I need to double down.

So I sent as many DMs as I could, every single day, to grow my community and expose more people to WriteStack and to my story.

I didn’t try to pitch anything. Honest conversations.

And that’s all it takes. Because when you solve a real pain, and you build honest connections, people trust you.

And if you solve their problem, they’ll **want** to be your subscriber.

## Last Two Months

Around 60 days ago, I jumped on a very eye\-opening call with [Michael Simmons](https://open.substack.com/users/75124283-michael-simmons?utm_source=mentions).

He told me that he really likes what I did with WriteStack.

We talked for a while and then he decided to try it with me, live on a Zoom call.

It was.. Horrible.

The UX was just bad. Many things didn’t work or were very hard to figure out and use.

I saw how his enthusiasm was wearing down.

After almost 2 hours, we ended the call. He told me that he’d love to help me, but the UX has to be **A LOT** better.

That’s when I figured out I have to stop adding more features and start improving the UX.

And honestly, it was one of the best decisions I made.

Looking back, I would give the version pre\-Michael maybe 4/10\.

A lot of tiny bugs, random errors that don’t provide any details to the user, Notes generated in a random language, too many items in the navbar.

A big mess.

Nowadays? I’d give it 9/10\.

And it shows. I have close to 70 paying subscribers, and maybe 1 bug report a week.

## **What’s Next**

In the next months, I am going to focus on marketing, as hard as I can.

That means:

1. Sending DMs daily
2. Writing daily notes
3. Keep sharing my journey
4. Seek feedback from as many people as I can
5. Offer demos to people who need WriteStack
6. Create onboarding emails to get people on WriteStack
7. Build in public (At least 1 note per day about WriteStack)

Here’s to a 3rd year, full of marketing and learning 🥂.

## Social Medias

Besides Substack, I dropped all the other social platforms.

I found LinkedIn to be too stressful and X to be too salesy and not authentic (hundreds of DMs in my inbox, trying to pitch me something).

Substack is the sweet spot.

People are extremely nice, everything feels authentic, and even generated content doesn’t feel robotic and generic.

Although I don’t like writing long\-form posts, I do enjoy the community.

So I had 3 newsletters

1. The IndiePreneur (Solopreneurship journey)
2. TechBooks (Tech book summaries)
3. The Writing Edge (Substack growth)

I moved all the TechBooks subscribers to The IndiePreneur (wrote about it [here](https://thewritingedge.substack.com/p/newsletter-growth-common-mistake)) and kept The Writing Edge.

Over a month since I imported the list, my subscribers count is still balancing.

For every post I put out I lose like 10\-30 subscribers that I imported from TechBooks, but overall I gain way more than I lose.
