I’ve been a solopreneur for almost 2 years.

And I’ve hit every wall you can imagine: burnout, ghost towns, products nobody gave a shit about.

If you're thinking about going solo, this’ll save you 650 days of suffering.

### 1\. Reading won’t save you. Only action does.

This is a hard lesson, but you must understand it.

NOBODY’s going to hold your hand through this journey. Even if you pay them, they can’t do the work for you.

You have to read, learn and most importantly \- **implement**.

You can read 1,000 books, buy 100 courses and attend 10 masterminds about marketing.

**If you don’t do the work yourself, nothing will click.**

Everything will feel like it’s far away from you and nothing will be truly clear and understood until you start doing the work yourself.

Only then will you **truly** understand.

### 2\. **Funnels print money. Build one or stay broke.**

Get ready, because when you read this **you will not be able to miss it again.**

Every time a creator gives you something for free in exchange for your email, guess what? You’re in their funnel.”

Let’s take Dickie Bush’s note ending, for example:

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EB3i!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff41f1e70-e357-4778-8f45-9c58e0f3c509_846x114.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EB3i!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff41f1e70-e357-4778-8f45-9c58e0f3c509_846x114.png)

These are called lead magnets. And he probably gives **TONS** of value in that crash course.

That free crash course? It’s bait, and it’s brilliant.

He delivers value, builds trust, and earns the right to sell. That’s how funnels *should* work.

And if you’re not doing it, you’re just hoping people buy because your product “deserves it”. They won’t.

### 3\. **No one cares about your feature. Start talking to humans.**

Around March of 2024 I found Marc Lou.

The guy made millions from one product, after shipping dozens that didn’t catch fire.

Here’s how his philosophy was understood: *“Ship fast and see if it sticks”*

In simple terms, build a product → Ship it → Did it get any traction? Good, double down. No traction? Kill it.

A cycle that takes 2\-4 weeks (1\-2 weeks with Cursor).

And I went by that philosophy for 600 days. Just shipping crappy products just to kill them a week later.

**This is the worst thing you can do.**

I wrote about it in depth in *[This mistake will cost you everything](https://theindiepreneur.substack.com/p/just-ship-it-bad-advice).*

Just to summarize it, the advice isn’t wrong. But if you ship without talking to users, you’re just playing indie startup roulette.

### 4\. **Ship an MVP. Then turn it into an MLP**

Your job is to validate your idea.

And the best way to do it is to build a real Minimum Viable Product. It can be riddled with bugs and problems.

You need to collect feedback, not get people to pay (YET).

Only when you feel like the idea is validated and that it solves people’s problem, turn it into a Minimum **Lovable** Product.

Make it delightful so first\-time users trust you.

Here’s how to think about it:

> MVP \= *intimate conversations* with a few early users.  
> MLP \= *cold encounters* with strangers who don’t care about your dreams.

Those early folks? They’ll forgive the mess.  
Strangers? They won’t. So make it lovable *then*, not before.

### 5\. **Shit happens. To everybody.**

Here’s a story on how I almost shut down WriteStack.

> It was Saturday night, a holiday in Israel (Mimuna). 16:00, everybody is preparing for the holiday dinner.
> 
> I am stressed the heck out. Datadog is on fire with errors and warnings from the past few hours.
> 
> **Notes schedules are not fired. Notes are not posted.**
> 
> It was when I had managed to get Tim Denning to use WriteStack and even give me a testimonial! 
> 
> All I am thinking is *“Tim is going to leave WriteStack and it’s going to die if I don’t find a solution”.*
> 
> The hours pass by. I am looking for a solution. Looking for ways to solve the goddamn errors.
> 
> It’s 18:00\. The scheduler works perfect locally. Nothing’s wrong. Then I realize the horrifying problem.
> 
> Substack added a bot\-detector.
> 
> When I sent the users’ notes from my server, I got a response back from cloudflare, telling me that they know I am a bot.
> 
> For hours I am trying to find workarounds. Just to keep Tim happy.
> 
> It’s 01:00 am. My girlfriend and I have our anniversary the next day. We already closed everything. And that’s when I realized:
> 
> It’s not going to work.

**What I learned**:

No matter how hard you plan, the platform you depend on can screw you over.

Always build with failure in mind. Redundancy, fallback options, and quick pivots.

TweetHunter had over 5k paying users. Then Twitter changed something that has blocked all the 5,000 users from using TweetHunter.

Did it kill the product? Hell no.

### 6\. **No one cares until you make them**

I’ll give it to you straight.

You’re not special. You’re not entitled to attention. Your first products are probably bad. People don’t care.

And that’s **completely okay.**

Think about it from your perspective, as a consumer. Do you care about a creator you’ve never heard of?

The answer is absolutely no.

You need to earn people’s care. You need to make them see you and want to know more about you.

Then they’ll start caring just a tiny bit.

How do you do that? You build. You show up. You write more, sharper, better. **You make them stop scrolling.**

And then you do it again. And again. Until they can’t ignore you anymore.

### 7\. **It’s okay to spend money**

When I started out, I was allergic to it.

I hunted for free tools. Even if something could save me *days*, I’d still waste time building it myself.

Why? Because I didn’t want to pay $20\.

That’s a fool’s mistake.

Not only does it waste days and weeks of your time, it also puts you in a bad mindset.

If you’re not willing to spend money on tools, why would anybody else pay for yours?

Let’s take the affiliate platform I use.

> It’s $50/month. In the past, I’d be cheap and build it all myself and run it manually.
> 
> Nowadays I understand it’s power.
> 
> Big affiliates don’t want to deal with amateurs. And not having a system that does everything automatically is exactly that.
> 
> Not only does it SCREAM amateur, it also wastes so much of your time.
> 
> And honestly, If it lands me just **5 loyal users**, it pays for itself *10x*.

It’s not a cost if it solves you a problem.

It’s a growth investment.

### 8\. **You don’t need a good product to make money**

This is something that I learned just recently. And it’s **damn powerful**.

Even though you do need to solve an aching problem to scale to big numbers, you don’t need it to start making money.

But to start making money? To get your first 10, 20, 50 customers? To get a few testimonials?

**You don’t need the perfect product.** Hell, you don’t even need a *good* one.

You need **marketing**.

Not fancy funnels, not paid ads with a perfect LTV/CAC. Just raw, scrappy, consistent visibility.

That means:

* Cold outreach
* Daily posting
* Showing your product to the *right people*
* Repeating it. Every. Damn. Day.

That’s how you make sales.

Build something basic, even half\-broken, and just market the hell out of it for 30 days.

You’ll be shocked.

### 9\. You can’t do everything.

When you start your journey as a solopreneur, everything is chaos.

You’ll have 50 ideas, 10 products you want to build, and a handful of platforms you want to grow on. And you’ll think that everything is doable. In parallel.

**This is an illusion.**

The problem isn’t that you can’t do it. It’s that you can’t do everything on a very high level. Not even well.

For example, you can take all your content from Substack and repurpose it on LinkedIn/X/Threads/YouTube etc.

But it’s not only about posting. It’s about building a community. About getting to know the platform, what works better and what doesn’t.

There are thousands of mediocre players out there.

**Our goal is to be uncommon amongst uncommon.**

Pick one idea. One product. One platform. Focus solely on those and you’ll see how fast things change.

### 10\. Gotta love the grind

This might sound like fluffy motivational talk, but it’s brutally true.

If you chase only the money or success, you’ll burn out fast. Because the money takes longer than expected, and success is far away.

You need to genuinely love the grind. Love the daily growth, the tiny breakthroughs, and even the fear and unknowns.

Because here’s the thing.

You might build something that blows up. You might, most likely, build a crappy solution.

Either way, the journey is going to be hard. Very hard.

But if you learn to be okay with the difficulty, you'll build strength, resilience, and wisdom far beyond what any quick success can offer.

Every hard day, every tough lesson, every setback.

Prepare to grind. There are so many people who try to do EXACTLY what you do.

The only thing that’s going to set you apart is how long will you endure and how fast will you implement.

### Final thought

Being a solopreneur is a tough road.

There will be blood. There will be silence. There will be days you question everything (More than you’d care to admit).

But if you stick around long enough, keep your head in the game, and focus on learning faster than the pain can hit, you’ll become unrecognizable.

Play the long game and enjoy the fight.
