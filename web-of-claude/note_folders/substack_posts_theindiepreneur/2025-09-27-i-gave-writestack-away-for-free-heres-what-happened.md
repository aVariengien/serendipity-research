Today, when you sign up to WriteStack, you have to put in your credit card.

But for an entire weekend, Friday 08:00 \- Sunday 23:59, WriteStack was free for everybody, no credit card required.

I ran the experiment because I know the value of WriteStack and I knew that if people would try it, they’d understand it as well.

And the results really surprised me.

And in today’s article, I am going to share every detail, lesson and mistake that I made.

But the first question to be answer is: **why?**

## Why I did a free weekend

I officially launched WriteStack on April and got my first ever payment on April 6th.

Ever since then the growth has been quite good.

I added many features, some of which were later removed some are widely used, I improved the UX and generally improved WriteStack.

As I was doing it, I tried to promote it in different way, which resulted in predictable growth.

But I didn’t do anything bold. And as I was walking one day, after a long period of a semi burnout, I came up with the idea.

That way I will have both try to improve the growth and attempt to break the burnout cycle.

And a free weekend is a fantastic way to do it.

It will keep me occupied for a few days to build everything for it, I will be occupied during the weekend and it’s a low risk high reward play.

---

## Setting the stage

Once I had the idea in mind, I knew I needed to build a checklist to complete before I can launch the event.

For an entire week I wrote code, emails and new tasks.

And the more I did, the more I realized how big of a thing it is and how many things I have to do before the weekend.

Here’s my finalized, big picture checklist:

#### 1\. Create an interest page

1. Add a new page that with an email input/sign up button
2. Make sure the page contains information about the event

#### 2\. Design the landing page for the weekend festivities

1. Create a banner
2. Change the pricing section
3. Make it look like christmas came early

#### 3\. Build a unique sign up flow that won’t require a credit card

1. Make sure that all the users that go through WriteStack during the weekend will get the special offer
2. Allow the option to convert to paid
3. (optional) Send the user to the pricing page and show a dialog that lets them claim their free weekend.

#### 4\. Write email timed broadcasts

1. A welcome email
2. Before the event starts to build the hype
3. During the event to help and guide users through
4. After the event to get feedback and convert users to paid

#### 5\. Prepare a dashboard

1. Write an analytics dashboard to monitor that stats of the weekend signups and conversions

Each of these tasks includes many subtasks, but it will give you and me a good direction for an event like this.

Here’s what actually happened during the free weekend:

## What I did wrong

Oof, there are plenty I did wrong.

And honestly, looking back, I think that it was a big failure on my end.

I didn’t promote it correctly, I rushed into it just to ‘do it’.

Let me list the top 3 things I did bad for you, so you don’t do them for your campaigns.

### Bad email sequences

An email sequence is a bunch of emails that are sent, based on timing, to a group of subscribers you define. Whenever a new subscriber joins, they start receiving the entire list, from the first email of the sequence.

For campaigns like this, it’s bad.

What happens is that a subscriber might join today, get the email that says that WriteStack is free in 5 days. Then, 2 days later, another one joins and gets the same email.

That’s bad and inconsistent. The better way to do it is with **timed broadcasts**.

That means that I create a list of emails that will be sent at specific times. If you were a subscriber when the schedule was triggered, you would’ve gotten the email.

If you were not, you won’t ever receive it.

That way everything is controlled and nobody gets an irrelevant email that’s outdated.

### Not enough tests

I didn’t run enough tests to make sure all the flows worked as expected, and a few people had a bad first experience.

For next time, I’ll set a few days to test everything thoroughly before committing.

Then, also, some flows were not very smooth.

For example, if you used WriteStack when the free weekend came to its end, you were thrown, without a warning, to the pricing page.

Although I put a friendly dialog there, it’s still not very pleasant.

### No follow ups

This is one of the things that probably made the weekend way less of a success than I thought.

I didn’t set up follow up emails after the weekend ended.

I didn’t even send follow up DMs until today, almost 2 weeks after the event was concluded.

---

## Final Thought

The free weekend was a great experience that taught me a lot.

I learned that traffic spikes mean nothing if the funnel leaks. I learned that without clean onboarding, proper follow\-ups, and tight promotion, you leave money on the table. And I learned that even when the numbers aren’t what you want, putting your product in front of fresh eyes forces you to face reality.

Although I feel like it was a failure, it was more of a validation that people *will* pay for WriteStack. Just not if you half\-ass the marketing.

Next time it will be much better!
