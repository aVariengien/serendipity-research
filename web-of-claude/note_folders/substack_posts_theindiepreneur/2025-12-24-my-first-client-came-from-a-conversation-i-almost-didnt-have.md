I spent months hiding my work.

I’d built my first website \- a simple directory for open source software \- and I was convinced it was garbage. Too rough. Too amateur. The kind of thing people would look at and think, “that’s cute, but...”

So I kept it to myself.

My wife saw it (she had to). She’s an amazing and talented graphic designer. But beyond that? Nobody.

Then one afternoon, I did something that terrified me.

I was at my brother\-in\-law’s place. He’s also a graphic designer. My wife and him are someone who actually know what good design looks like. The worst possible audience for my scrappy first project.

But something pushed me to pull out my laptop, open the website, and turn the screen toward him.

What happened next changed everything.

## **The Long Road to That Moment**

Let me back up.

I’d been thinking about learning to code for years. I love software \- everything about it fascinates me. But thinking and doing are very different things.

In 2024, I finally decided to stop thinking and start building.

The problem? I had no idea where to begin.

What tools should I use? Where do you even buy domains? What do half these code snippets actually do? The questions felt endless.

I discovered Windsurf and started watching what other people were building with it. That got me excited enough to try.

Was I slow? Absolutely.

Too slow to start. Too slow to prompt. Too slow to make decisions. Every step felt like wading through mud.

But I kept going, because for the first time, I was actually *building something*.

I decided on a simple directory website \- a place to discover open source software. Nothing fancy. Just functional.

A couple months of working on\-and\-off later, I had something.

---

## **The Fear of Showing Up**

Here’s the thing nobody tells you about building your first project:

> **Finishing isn’t the hard part. Showing people is.**

I’d look at my website and see every flaw. The spacing that wasn’t quite right. The features I couldn’t figure out how to add. The overall “first attempt” energy radiating from every pixel.

Around September, I finally bought the domain: **[prosperinoss.com](http://prosperinoss.com/)**

(The data pulls automatically from GitHub once a day \- that was the last feature I managed to add. Feel free to check it out. It’s changed a bit since then, though I haven’t touched it much this year.)

Putting it live felt like progress. But I still wasn’t telling anyone about it.

The site existed. I just pretended it didn’t.

---

## **The Laptop Moment**

So there I was at my brother\-in\-law’s place.

My laptop was in my backpack. The website was live. And for some reason I still can’t fully explain, I decided this was the moment.

I pulled out the laptop.

Opened the browser.

Loaded prosperinoss.com.

Turned the screen toward him.

And waited for the polite disappointment.

Instead, he said: **“Oh wow, is this your first website ever?”**

I braced myself. “Yes.”

**“It looks amazing! I love the animations and the design. I think it looks pretty good!”**

I was stunned. We talked about the site for a few minutes \- how I built it, what tools I used, what I was trying to accomplish.

Then I was about to start packing up my laptop.

That’s when he said the thing that changed my trajectory:

**“Hey, one of my good friends has been asking if I know anyone who can build them a website and an app. Do you think you could do it?”**

---

## **The Leap**

My brain immediately said: *You have no idea how to build what they need.*

My mouth said: **“Yes, I can definitely try.”**

Here’s what I want you to understand about that moment:

I genuinely did not know how to build what they were asking for. My only experience was one directory website I’d cobbled together over months. An app? For a real client? With actual expectations?

I was terrified.

But I said yes anyway.

Long story short: I figured it out. The customer loved the product. I got paid for work I didn’t know I could do until I was already doing it.

All because I showed my brother\-in\-law a website I was embarrassed about.

---

## **What I Learned**

The lesson here isn’t just “show your work.”

It’s more specific than that:

**The person you’re most afraid to show your work to might bring you your first opportunity.** And it’s maybe not related to software at first. It just could be your thoughts that you want to put out on social media.

I wasn’t networking. I wasn’t pitching. I wasn’t running a marketing campaign.

I was just surviving the vulnerability of showing something imperfect to someone whose opinion I respected.

That moment of exposure \- the one I almost avoided \- became my first opportunity.

Your mind will race with stress and doubts. That’s normal. That’s actually the signal that you might be on the right path.

The fear doesn’t go away. You just learn to move forward anyway and move the fear to the side.

---

## **The Takeaway**

If you’re sitting on a project you think isn’t ready, ask yourself:

**Ready for what?**

My website wasn’t ready for a design award. It wasn’t ready for a Product Hunt launch. But it was ready enough to show one person \- and that one person connected me to my first paying client.

You don’t need perfect.

You need *one conversation*. One response. That’s it. It would shift your thinking into: “Wait, this whole thing is actually possible.”

And you won’t get that conversation if your laptop stays in your backpack.
