*And that's perfectly fine*

If your product doesn’t have any traction, it doesn’t matter how beautifully you wrap it. Adding extra animations or implementing a slick design won’t change your core offer.

#### Here are the main takeaways if you’re short on time:

Before you release a project, make sure to consider the following:

* Have your design reviewed and tested by users. Get feedback and improve if needed before moving to development.

	+ **Pro tip:** In 99% of the cases, there’s no reason not to go mobile first. Have your responsive design developed first because most people will probably open it from their phone.
* Have each big feature tested, if possible, before you fix bugs and try to perfect it.
* Always remember: **It’s better to release a buggy product that nobody loves than to release a fully polished product that nobody loves.**

If people don’t have any use for the feature, or if you can’t show them why they do have a use for it, they won’t use it, whether it was designed by a Spotify UX designer or if it is buggy and slow.

How do I know? **Personal pain**.

#### Background

In December of ‘23, [Anton Zaides](https://open.substack.com/users/121956618-anton-zaides?utm_source=mentions) and I started a new project together related to books.  
We both were fed up with the way Goodreads looks and that there’s no decent alternative.

So, we decided to make a new website that will provide a much better UX and give the users all the main features they used to have in Goodreads and more. We called it BookWizard.

Since neither of us has any experience with website design, we turned to UpWork to find a professional designer. And we settled on the second one.

*Note: in future newsletters, I will elaborate on why you should not settle for a professional.*

We worked on it for over 3 months before the first release. And then, something happened.

#### The realization

Before we get to the juice, keep in mind that we had a budget of \~$4000 and we spent almost half of it only on the designer (**$2000**).  
The results were beautiful, but… The feedback was almost unanimous after we initially released it to a dozen testers:

*The design is beautiful, **but not practical**.*

People just didn’t get the vibe. Apparently, people want to feel warm and cozy when they think about books. Our website was futuristic and “cyberish”, as one of the respondents said.

Some didn’t even know what the website was supposed to be doing, even though Anton did an incredible job explaining it.

#### The problem(s)

1. We spent too much time with the designer and not with people. We had a full suite of designs, desktop and mobile, every screen and extras.
2. Then, not only did we not show it to people, but we started developing and perfecting it with animations and cool transitions.

**It took us over 3 months to show something to someone.**

#### The conclusion

**Don’t sweat the small stuff before you have a feature that’s proven.**

What does it mean?

Settle on a short list of features to test your app.  
Have a very minimal project with a very minimal design. Let people test it, feel it and give you feedback on how to proceed.

Heck, even before implementing anything, let people see the design (Preferably play with it in Figma) to get the vibe and tell you if it feels good or not. **This step alone would save us hundreds if not thousands of dollars and months of development.**

Our problem was that we wasted too much time implementing “Necessary features”, which cost us a whole month of development (Half the time it took us to write the whole app).

Always keep in mind:   
**It’s better to release a buggy product that nobody loves than to release a fully polished product that nobody loves.**

In both cases, you get the bad news that your product is not good enough, but in the first case you save months of development.

So if you have a working product and it’s buggy, it’s completely fine. Let people guide you to what to do next.
