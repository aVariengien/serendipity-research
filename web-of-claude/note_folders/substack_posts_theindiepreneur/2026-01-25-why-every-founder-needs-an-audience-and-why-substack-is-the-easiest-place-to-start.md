# Why Every Founder Needs an Audience (and Why Substack Is the Easiest Place to Start)

Published: 2026-01-25
Source: https://theindiepreneur.substack.com/p/why-every-founder-needs-an-audience

---

In 600 days I started 12 startups and made $0 dollars, 0 users

It’s not that I didn’t work hard or didn’t have the discipline. My problem was that I built alone. I had a consistent loop that I did over and over again, thinking that it will one day work:

> I came up with a ‘genius idea’ → I built it → I thought I made progress → I launched launch → Nobody came → I made no money → I felt awful → You I burnt out → Repeat.

If you’re a founder who just wants to build products and figure out “distribution” later, I get it. I believed the same thing. And it was the most expensive mistake I made.

## **The lie founders love to believe (me included)**

There’s this comforting idea most early\-stage founders cling to:

> *“If the product is good, people will come.”*

It sounds completely logical. I mean, if it’s a solution people need and it works well, they should come, right?

Wrong. They don’t. And the reason is pretty simple.

Good products don’t magically attract users. They’re discovered, explained, trusted, and refined by **USERS**. Without users, you’re just hoping what you built works good enough and solves an issue.

I launched multiple products (12\). I shipped features(countless).

What I didn’t get:

* customers
* revenue
* signals

What I did get was this creeping thought most founders get at one point or another:

*“Maybe I’m just not good enough.”*

## **The real cost of building without an audience**

When you don’t have an audience, it’s almost impossible to verify your idea. I mean, you need people to want to spend their time on your shitty product. And it’s hard.

You spend months, sometimes years, building the wrong things for the wrong people. And with every product you feel like *‘THIS IS THE ONE!’*

For me, that was roughly 600 days.

Six hundred days of effort without direction. Six hundred days of building without confidence. **Six hundred days I can’t get back.**

## **Why building alone breaks founders**

You, as a founder, need a little less unknowns.

When you put something out and someone replies, agrees, disagrees, asks a question, your motivation stays high. When you work hard and nothing comes back, motivation decays.

Founders experience more burnout than anybody else because they live in the unknown. And it’s MUCH harder when you don’t even get feedback for what you do.

Eventually, you wonder if *you’re* the problem. That’s why good builders quit.

## **Audience isn’t marketing**

Here’s the reframe that would’ve saved me years:

An audience is for verification.

When people follow you, you can test ideas before building. You get reactions. You *build* the product before you ever sell it.

Once you have an audience, something shifts.

People know you. They’ve seen how you think. They follow you for a reason.

So when you ask a question, they answer. And when you want to test a solution, they ***WANT*** to help you.

You become someone they already trust.

## **Why substack is the easiest place to start**

I don’t actually care which platform you use.

What matters is ownership.

Substack works because you build a community and you **own** the email list. That combination is everything. Email means you can reach people directly. You’re not at the mercy of algorithms. You’re building possible leads, not just engagement.

Think about it. If X decides to ban you for any reason, you lose EVERYTHING you built.

With email \+ community, you can ask what people struggle with. Validate ideas before writing code. Make money without a big launch.

Directly. Get to everybody, without hoping your post is good enough to reach them.

## **Final word**

There are two paths in front of you.

Path one: Keep building alone and tell yourself you’ll market later. Launch when it’s “ready.” Hear nothing. Repeat.

Path two: Start writing and sharing what you’re thinking and building. Get people to follow you along and give you feedback. Build momentum while you build products, so once you find a good solution, you won’t need to market as hard.
