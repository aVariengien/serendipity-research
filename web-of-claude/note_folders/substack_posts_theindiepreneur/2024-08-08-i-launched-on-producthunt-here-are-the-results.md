# The verification

Before we started developing anything, we searched everywhere to look for a competition and figure out what’s their strong and weak points.

After several days of that (Not days of work. I’d assume we had spent 4\-6 hours total), we figured out there was no real competition in the space and that the traffic was quite good.

One more thing we figured out is that most websites had a very old UI and a very bad UX. We figured that this could be our point of strength.

We were read to hire a good designer to get things going.

**P.S.**  
After a few weeks of development, Anton read about a website that does exactly what we were planning, and does it fantastically.

# The first iteration

For the first MVP, Anton and I wanted to have a book backlog manager, to which you can import your books easily.

### Features:

1. To read/Read backlogs management system
2. Import from Goodreads
3. Import from CSV
4. Have a database of books for easy search

In order to be better than the competition and have a great UX, we wanted the imports to be automatic and have a great search experience.

For example, if you want to import your books from goodreads, in any website you need to export your books from Goodreads and upload the excel sheet you downloaded.

We had a much better idea.  
We found a way to scrape all your Goodreads books with only your profile name.

Long story short, we spent weeks on this feature, which eventually didn’t see the light of day.

Why? We’ll get to that in the pivot section.

**P.S.**  
Same story for the database of books.

### The design

After many discussions, we decided to go with a desktop first version because we assumed most people will access the website through their computer.

> (That was half true. Around 50% of the traffic was from the computer.)

We hired an expensive designer who designed a beautiful website. We got to work and developed everything.

#### The problem

We spent months building the first version without asking anybody what they think about the design. And let me tell you, it was a disaster.

It didn’t vibe with concept of books. It felt to alienating when it was supposed to give you a warm, fuzzy feeling.

It didn’t work on mobile. At all.

# The pivot

Now, at this point we realized we need to change things completely.

* Design a website for books and not cyber
* Have a mobile design first approach
* Lock on minimum, core features

Also, around this point Anton figured out he can’t keep up with the demands of the development of BookWiz. He had a kid, a wife and a full\-time job.

> If you’re interested in the full story, go ahead and read [How a 6 months partnership ended](https://theindiepreneur.substack.com/p/how-a-6-months-partnership-ended).

We hired a new designer. This time a cheaper one, so we can iterate on the design as many times as needed and stay within budget.

### Features:

Instead of having a regular books manager, Anton came up with the idea of shareable lists.

The user can create a list of books that he likes and share it with his audience.  
These lists would also be recommended to other users, with a matching algorithm we can up with.

That way we don’t have to deal with the competition out there because we have a brand new product.

So here’s the new list:

1. Create shareable books lists
2. Books matching algorithm
3. Backlog manager

> Looking back now, we didn’t simplify anything during the process. We only added more features and more complexity before verifying anything.

---

From here on out we built BookWiz and iterated with the designer until [the end of the partnership](https://theindiepreneur.substack.com/p/how-a-6-months-partnership-ended). Then we both stopped developing it until 2 weeks before the ProductHunt release.

#### **API Gateway\+Lambda**

→ For the backend functions and authenticated requests.

#### **RDS**

→ The postgresql database. Anton and I assumed that a relational DB will be the best solution for our product.

#### **IAM**

→ Manage security within AWS by controlling who is authenticated (signed in) and authorized (has permissions) to use resources.

#### **Cognito \+ Amplify**

→ User authentication.

#### **S3 \+ CloudFront \+ Certificate Manager**

→ As a website that relies mostly on the images shown, we needed a CDN to deliver the images as fast as possible to the client.

The Certificate Manager is for the bookwiz.app ownership authorization, so we could use cdn.bookwiz.app as our cdn url.

#### **EventBridge**

→ The product involves scraping Goodreads data to improve our database. Since a lambda has 15 minutes timeout and we had tens of thousands of books to scrape and avoid getting error 429 (Too many requests), we had to limit the amount of requests per minute.

So we used EventBridge to schedule tasks, so that every 15 minutes a new scrape would run.

# Improvements for next project

### Product side

1. **Verify. As soon as possible.**  
→ Don’t build the whole project before you let someone test it. Try to verify the idea by talking to people and see what they think about it. After reading “The Mom Test”, I wrote an article [How to quickly verify your idea](https://theindiepreneur.substack.com/p/how-to-deploy-fast)
2. **Minimum core feature first.**  
→ Don’t overcomplicate things. Decide on a core feature, which should be as minimal as possible to deliver the value you want to deliver.

> In BookWiz case, the MVP should have been either a backlog manager, shareable lists or a recommendation system. Not all three together.

3. **Go mobile\-first design. 99\.9% of the times.**→ Desktop design on mobile is ugly at best.  
Mobile design on desktop is ugly at worst.

### Tech side

1. **Don’t overcomplicate things**→ Using complex services before you have any validation to your idea is a waste of time. Get people to use your product, listen to their feedback and then consider adding complicated features.
2. **Build free. Upgrade later if needed**→ There’s no reason not to build free. You have anything you need to verify your initial idea for free. If you can’t find something for free, [hit me up](https://www.linkedin.com/in/orel-zilberman-225a37137/) and I’ll help you find it.
3. **Have a template**

Every new project I create, I learn something new.   
Be it as small as learning how to authenticate with NextJS middleware to learning how to configure DNS records.  
  
In order to have this at the ready for whenever I need it, I have a template project.  
It includes everything a SaaS product needs, besides the core feature.  
  
So whenever I start a new project, 95% of the work is building the core feature. 5% is usually learning something new or adjusting the template.  
  
Trust me, **having a template will save you hundreds of hours of development.**
