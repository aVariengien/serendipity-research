# The good

## You own your time

**Having the freedom to do what ever comes to mind is priceless.  
And it’s worth almost everything.**

Being your own boss and running things the way your creative mind wants **gives you the rush of waking up early and stay up until late to get things done.**

**You no longer need to ask for vacation time and hope someone will grant you a few weeks of freedom.**

Although your time is more constraint, **it feels 100x times better to know that YOU choose what you can and cannot do.**

**You get to work from wherever you want,** however you want and for how long you want, **regardless if someone approves a remote work or not.**

And the best thing about it? You get the potential to keep doing it for the rest of your life.

Legend has it that if you do that for long enough you will join a secret cult called FIRE.  
I don’t really care about the RE, though. I want the FI.

# The bad

## You own your time

Being your own boss comes with strings attached. Some are good. Some are not.

**You spend dozens, if not hundreds of hours building something just to watch everybody ignore it** and realize you didn’t do a good work figuring out whether that’s a product people actually want or just your fantasy.

**You have to learn to live with the unknowns of the independent life.**

\- Am I good enough for this journey?  
\- Should I go back to find a job and give up?  
\- Will I ever be able to sustain myself like this?

\- Will this project work out?  
\- What should I work on next?  
\- Is this a good use of my time?

And you need to have answers to most of them, especially those that might undermine your journey. Or at least have the self\-confidence to tell yourself what you wish the answers would be.

**The feeling of boredom creeps in from time to time.**  
And you get nervous because you are bored and you know that you need to make money asap, otherwise you’ll end up in the jobs market again.

> **Tip for boredom:** Lean into it.  
> Fighting it will only make it worse and letting it be can help you generate great ideas and experience new things.

To be honest, most of these are true to those who have a job as well.  
The main difference is that you don’t get paid to feel those feelings as a solopreneur. That’s why it’s so much harder.

# The ugly

## You own your time

**You are mostly alone in this journey.**

As a solopreneur, you spend most of your time at home, working, sleeping and, in my case, being with your partner.

But the feeling of loneliness does not come from that. It comes from the fact that you don’t have anyone to understand your feelings and help you push through.

So unless you have a good friend in the same boat, you need to learn how to handle things in your mind alone. Or seek professional help, which is also a viable option, yet an expensive one.

**You may experience extreme burn out cycles**

I have recently climbed out of a deep pit of burn out.  
And I wrote about it twice in the last 4 weeks.

The feeling of not wanting to wake up in the morning and doubting everything that you do.

You feel depressed and consider ending the solopreneurship journey and just getting a comfortable job that pays well.

*By the way, I started biting my nails just writing about the topic of burn out.*

And once you fall into this pit, it’s a hard climb to get out.   
I wrote an article on how I try to get out of these cycle on [How to deal with burnout](https://theindiepreneur.substack.com/p/how-to-deal-with-burnout).

In addition to that, I found that **writing** and **reading** are very helpful.

Writing forced me to figure out what bothers me and organize the mess I had in the head.

Reading stories of people like David Goggins really helped me see and push through the pain and hard times, knowing there’s a better me on the other end of it.

# Final words

Although I ended on a harsh note, I will repeat what I said in the beginning:

**Having the freedom to do what ever comes to mind is priceless.  
And it’s worth almost everything.**

Even though I had experienced some awful times and felt bad for long stretches of time, having the **freedom** to feel those feelings and learn more about yourself and grow from it is worth it all.

So if you want it bad enough, give it a go.

Worst case? You spent a few months learning, growing and experiencing new feelings.  
Best case? You have a lifetime of freedom. Freedom of choice.
