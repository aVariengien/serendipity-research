# Lessons I've learned from my first product release

*And they will save you a lot of time.*

Published: 2024-02-13
Source: https://theindiepreneur.substack.com/p/lessons-ive-learned-from-my-first

---

Around 3 months ago I started a new project with [Anton Zaides](https://open.substack.com/users/121956618-anton-zaides?utm_source=mentions) , which is a website for book readers that provides tools and features at a level no other website does.  
Our vision was to simplify how readers manage their book collections, including read lists, to\-read lists, favorites, and more, with an easy import feature from Goodreads.

#### **Number \#1:** Don’t sweat the small stuff.

Initially, we aimed to include an automatic Goodreads list import feature. Unfortunately, we encountered too many edge cases that caused us to delay the release for another 3\.5 weeks, only to find out that nobody cared.  
There were too many other flaws, and it was not even remotely important, which leads me to my next point.

We learned from it to focus on the core things first before we spend weeks perfecting the nice to\-haves.

#### **Number \#2:** Test, test, test!

We always knew we needed to release something as soon as possible to understand if we were heading in the right direction or not. We had it in our minds since day 1\.

Yet, as you go you get too attached to the project and you feel like there are must\-haves before the release, so you get into rabbit holes that take a few days in the best case to get out of.

So what should you do? Have a small set of very basic features and another one that delivers value that’s different from your competition.  
In our example, all we needed was a very basic import option, login and CRUD(Create, Read, Update, Delete) functions for books and backlogs.  
And the feature that delivers value? We believed then that it was going to be the UX.

#### Number \#3: Check your competition

That’s one thing we did well. We looked everywhere to find a website that resembled our idea and delivered what we wanted to deliver.

A few weeks into the project, we randomly stumbled upon a website that had most of the features that we wanted. This was such a blow to the motivation. We thought that it was the end.

It took us some time to understand that that’s what it takes to be a part of this world. Build a good product, fend off competition and do the best you can. So, we decided to move on and keep building

#### Number \#4: Drop the Jira.

We started out using Jira to manage our tasks. We would diligently have a weekly meeting to set it up, discuss new tasks, have a description with definition\-of\-done and have each other go over the other’s tasks to approve it.   
It didn’t take more than maybe 3\-4 weeks til we stopped using it.

The reason it’s not helpful so early is because we are 2 people who communicate everything over Slack, every day. Every issue that arises is discussed and settled very fast. Therefore, spending hours completing the Jira is better used for coding and coming up with ideas.

#### Number \#5: **Hire someone who gets your vision. And preferably, who speaks your language.**

One major mistake that we made was hiring a designer who did not fully comprehend what we wanted to achieve.  
Don’t get me wrong, his designs were beautiful and we were mesmerized by them. And that’s the problem. We couldn’t see that the UX was bad for our users. Even for us to use.  
That mistake cost us around $2000\.

So next time we hire someone, we will make sure that he has some sort of connection to books and that he completely gets our vision and even offers ideas of his own.

#### **Key Takeaways:**

* Focus on what truly matters to your users.
* Launch early with essential features to gather feedback.
* Understand your competition but don't be discouraged by it.
* Don’t use tools like Jira to manage your tasks. You need to deliver as fast as possible.
* Hire someone who gets your vision, comes up with ideas of his own and, preferably, speaks your language.
