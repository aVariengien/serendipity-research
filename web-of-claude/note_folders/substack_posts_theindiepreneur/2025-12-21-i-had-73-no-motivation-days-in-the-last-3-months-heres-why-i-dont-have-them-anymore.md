## Most days, I don’t feel like doing anything

A couple weeks ago, on a Wednesday, I sat at home and felt absolutely nothing.

No drive, no excitement. Didn’t even go work out or leave the room.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!33ES!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F24d8a91d-c644-4c21-8d1c-0e026ada2144_400x227.gif)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!33ES!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F24d8a91d-c644-4c21-8d1c-0e026ada2144_400x227.gif)

I stared at my screen, procrastinating and scrolling. I knew I had to do something. Anything, just to keep the ball rolling.

So I made one decision:  
Today’s *lever* is writing notes. Nothing else matters.

So I closed everything but WriteStack, and started writing Notes.  
I set up a 2 hours timer and started cranking them. It wasn’t as productive as I hoped it’d be. I wrote like 30 Notes, which usually take me 30\-45 minutes.

But hey! Progress is progress.

But when I was done, the week’s schedule was covered. I can allow myself some time off now.

I was productive that day.

## What People Tell Themselves About Motivation

People love saying things like:

* “You just need to rest.”
* “You’re probably not aligned.”
* “Motivation will come back.”

Sometimes that’s true. But most of the times it’s just bullshit. Motivation is not something you *wait for*. It’s something you **generate by moving**.

I had to accept this:

> Low motivation isn’t the problem.  
> **No process for low motivation times is the problem.**

when I don’t have a plan the day before, or I don’t plan for these bad days, I just sit down in front of the computer, and wonder what I should do next because I have no idea.

If you don’t decide *in advance* what you do on bad days, bad days decide for you.

And they’re terrible decision\-makers.

---

## Bad days aren’t dangerous. *Undefined* bad days are.

Here’s where people get stuck. They wake up tired and ask:

> “What do I *feel* like doing today?”

Or worse

> “What do I *need* to do today?”

On low energy days my brain defaults to choosing:

— Comfort  
— Distraction  
— Fake productivity  
— Or nothing at all

And that’s how I end up doing literally nothing, promising that I will get better tomorrow and move on to do absolutely nothing.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bQMA!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_lossy/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F415491bc-44c4-4b17-8813-b7799d814c8a_480x400.gif)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bQMA!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F415491bc-44c4-4b17-8813-b7799d814c8a_480x400.gif)

So I created a rule. The Lever Rule.

## The Lever Rule: How to stay productive

My coach told me something that stuck:

> Every day, you have a few **levers** you can pull.  
> Your only job is to choose *which one* will generate the highest ROI.

Some days, that lever grows revenue that takes a lot of effort and focus.  
Some days it’s a small action to keep the ball rolling.

I spent a full week building an activity feed for WriteStack.

It’s crazy useful and I love it. The responses are also great. I even enjoyed building it.

But sales dropped about 50% during that period.

Was it the “right” lever to pull? Probably not. But that’s the result of not having a “bad day” plan.

When you have a bad day plan, you can do the things that you need to do and then continue working on what you’re enjoying without feeling bad.

When you don’t, you just do the opposite.

You work on the things that won’t push the business forward (as much) and then you feel bad about it.

## My non\-negotiable daily minimum (Steal this)

Every day, I have three tasks that I want accomplish:

1. **High\-energy task**  
Writing an article. Takes 2–3 hours. Annoying. Important.
2. **Mid\-energy task**  
Writing notes and scheduling them with **WriteStack**.  
Still requires thinking, but way lighter.
3. **Low\-energy task**  
Sending direct messages. No creativity. Just discipline.

On decent days, I do all three.

On bad days I **always do at least one**, preferably the

That’s it.

This is why low days are not an issue for me anymore.

## Waiting for motivation is procrastination

It’s as simple as this:

**Waiting to feel motivated is just procrastination with better branding.**

If you wait, nothing moves and you lose momentum. And the worst thing is you find yourself starting over. *Again*.

If you do something, even a tiny thing, you move the needle. Your habits stay strong and your progress. Look, I’m under\-motivated 60–70% of the time. But I still write content. Still grow. **Still move forward**.

Because I decided what “enough” looks like on bad days.
