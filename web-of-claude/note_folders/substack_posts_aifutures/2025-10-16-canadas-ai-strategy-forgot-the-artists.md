# Canada’s AI Strategy Forgot the Artists

*Dangerous mistake dudes*

Published: 2025-10-16
Source: https://aifutures.substack.com/p/canadas-ai-strategy-forgot-the-artists

---

Canada has an ambitious [national AI strateg](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/ai-strategy/en)y. It invests in research, it grows talent pipelines, it builds compute infrastructure and talks about safety, regulation, and commercialization. All of that is important — but it misses something absolutely fundamental.

It forgets the artists.

Dudes, we need to talk about this. There’s no real mention of art, culture, creativity, or the people and practices that help us make meaning. Our museums, libraries, archives, design studios, performance spaces, and cultural organizations barely register in the plan. Artists themselves don’t appear at all. I’m pretty sure this is not just an oversight and that it belies a serious strategic error of the kind (I’m sorry to say) we’ve made before. If Canada wants to shape the age of AI we need artists at the centre of how we build, deploy, and understand this technology.

## Strategy that’s strong and narrow, like a plastic ruler that has a logo from a mining company on it

The *[Pan\-Canadian AI Strategy](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/ai-strategy/en)* is designed around three pillars: Commercialization, Standards, and Talent and Research. Together they paint a picture of a country trying to lead in machine learning, create new companies, and build regulatory frameworks that keep people safe.

But where’s culture. Remember culture? It’s where we experiment with ideas, prototype futures, and explore meaning. Well, here it’s treated as completely irrelevant to the development of AI. Artists, designers, and cultural practitioners are nowhere to be found in the story Canada is telling us about our technological future.

This strikes hard especially right now, when we are in a historic battle for imaginative firepower that will help us ask the hard questions about who and what technology is for. Culture is where we invent ourselves, invent new forms, challenge narratives, create visions that connect us, make visible what is invisible.

In the cultural sector we are used to piping up from the back, addressing our dude\-sector overlords with a limp request to be included. This time we can’t do it that way \- it’s not about representation, it’s imagination, and without that, how can we slay?

## Hey look over there!

Before you start thinking \- what a cute vision but it’s not practical! Let’s look at how Europe is doing it differently.

Launched in **2015**, the [European Commission’s](https://starts.eu/) **[STARTS](https://starts.eu/)** [initiative](https://starts.eu/) directly embeds artists with industrial R \& D teams in scientific labs. And no, they’re not there to create tinfoil decorations, they are there to change the questions.

Another example \- the **[European ARTificial Intelligence Lab](https://ars.electronica.art/ailab/en/)**. Representing 13 major cultural organizations and led by Ars Electronica, the network brings artists into research institutions to probe the social, ethical, and philosophical dimensions of AI, “focusing on aspects beyond the technological and economic horizon to scrutinize cultural, psychological, philosophical and spiritual aspects". These projects treat artistic experimentation as essential to, rather than separate from, innovation.

At the infrastructure level, initiatives like **[Cultural Gems](https://knowledge4policy.ec.europa.eu/projects-activities/cultural-gems_en)** and the forthcoming **[European Collaborative Cloud for Cultural Heritage (ECCCH)](https://research-and-innovation.ec.europa.eu/research-area/social-sciences-and-humanities/cultural-heritage-and-cultural-and-creative-industries-ccis/cultural-heritage-cloud_en)** are building machine\-readable cultural data infrastructures. This means making cultural collections, archives, and heritage materials visible to AI so that they become active, governable resources that artists, institutions, and communities can work with.

**[Creative Europe](https://culture.ec.europa.eu/creative-europe)** [and](https://culture.ec.europa.eu/creative-europe) **[Horizon Europe](https://culture.ec.europa.eu/creative-europe)** now fund calls that require culture\-tech collaboration as part of the brief.

I won’t even mention the zillion labs, festivals, foundations, and other hybrid initiatives like **[Medialab Matadero](https://www.medialab-matadero.es/en/activities)** (where artists, technologists, citizens, and policymakers co\-create digital projects to explore emergent conditions), Berlin’s **[LAS Art Foundation](https://www.las-art.foundation/)** (that commissions ambitious works at the intersection of art, AI, and quantum computing), Rotterdam’s **[V2\_ Institute for the Unstable Media](https://v2.nl/)**, Amsterdam’s **[Sonic Acts](https://sonicacts.com/)** festival, the **[Edinburgh Futures Institute](https://efi.ed.ac.uk/)**, and so many others, that sustain long\-term, cross\-disciplinary experimentation in a way that is meaningfully supported by public funds on the basis that they advance understanding and create knowledge. We have some of those things in Canada of course but you wouldn’t know it looking at the strategy map.

## We’ve been here before and we know how it ends

This isn’t the first time Canada has sidelined artists during a technological shift.

In the early days of digital media, public funding prioritized film, television, and commercial “convergent media” \- things the people in charge thought had the potential to make money out of the gate. I probably don’t have to point out that it didn’t work: we did not end up with 400 new billion\-dollar children’s entertainment franchises (was that the goal?) nor did we end up rocking the world. [Independent visual and media artists — the ones experimenting with the aesthetics and politics of new technologies — were left out](https://aifutures.substack.com/p/i-was-there). Funding models rewarded export potential and market readiness, not creative exploration. Infrastructure was built for short\-term outputs not long\-term capacity.

##### Grantwriting season

The result was predictable: collaboration across sectors was stunted, artists were excluded from innovation ecosystems, and much of Canada’s most forward\-looking cultural work was forced to operate at the margins or just collapse into a pile of black denim.

We can’t afford to repeat that mistake. Generative AI is a cultural force that is reshaping how we create, communicate, and understand the world, and treating it like a customer acquisition tool for businesses in the industrial fasteners and screws distribution vertical isn’t going to do it. If artists aren’t part of that process, the story Canada tells about AI will be narrower, more commercial, and less imaginative than it could be.

## What Canada could do differently right now

None of this is impossible. And actually one of the reasons I’ve been thinking about this again right now is that [Canada has opened a call for input on AI strategy](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/ised/en/public-consultations/help-define-next-chapter-canadas-ai-leadership), which is fabulous.

Here are some ideas for you:

* **Create a Canada Generative Arts Fund.** Launch a national program to support AI\-based art, speculative media, and emergent creative forms — from participatory AI performances to adaptive narratives and generative scenography. Do not force it into a subset of artistic practice by making it a small program administered by the Canada Council for the Arts.
* **Establish permanent AI Culture Labs.** Build interdisciplinary spaces where artists, technologists, scientists, and communities collaborate on long\-term research and production, co\-governed by cultural organizations and other partners.
* **Build a Cultural Data Commons.** Develop national infrastructure for cultural datasets — from archives and heritage materials to open collections — with ethical governance, Indigenous data sovereignty, and machine\-readable standards.
* **Include culture in compute access.** Reserve public AI compute for museums, libraries, archives, and cultural organizations so they can experiment and innovate.
* **Make AI literacy a cultural mission.** Fund museums and libraries to become public hubs for AI education and creative experimentation.
* **Recognize cultural data and benefit\-sharing.** Build frameworks for attribution, revenue sharing, and collective licensing when cultural data trains AI models.
* **Write creativity into the Pan\-Canadian AI Strategy.** Make artistic experimentation a pillar of the national plan alongside Commercialization, Standards, and Talent and Research or at the very least give it it’s own supercluster.

# Artists are not some kind of weird luxury add\-on

At the heart of this conversation is a deeper question: What kind of future do we want to build?

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2oZ_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6b8230de-ad58-4495-8432-46a6f6cdd866_1024x1024.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2oZ_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6b8230de-ad58-4495-8432-46a6f6cdd866_1024x1024.png)

##### THIS is a weird luxury add\-on

Do we see artists as peripheral — people who come in with felt pens after the real innovation is done, or do we recognize them as essential to how societies imagine, prototype, and build futures? Do we treat cultural data as a commodity to be mined, or as a shared inheritance that requires consent, care, and creativity? Do we design AI systems that replicate existing power structures, or do we invite artists to help us imagine new ones?

If Canada wants to matter in the next century of technological and cultural change, we need to get busy putting culture at the centre. Right now our national AI strategy is building a powerful machine and we need to make sure it’s a good machine that facilitates great things, not a great machine that facilitates boring things, but without artists at the table we risk forgetting why the machine mattered in the first place.
