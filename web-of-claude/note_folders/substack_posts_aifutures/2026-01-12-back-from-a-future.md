At the beginning of this new year it seems appropriate to have just returned from Shanghai, a city that is living in a future. A big fat one. I say “a” future not “the” future because there are so many aspects of that city that are deeply unimaginable to my brain, coming from a North American, Canadian, especially a British Columbian position, that it’s incomprehensible to think we might see that kind of future here, at least on the matter of culture, at least with the approaches we have been seeing to date.

It was my first time in Shanghai so my immediate impressions include all the things you might expect. Holy shit, there are 30 million people in Shanghai, about as many as in Canada, and another 10 million in the city you’ve never heard of that is 150km away. OMG check out the scale of the architecture and the inclusion of bulbous towers, ubiquitous media facades, electric vehicles, and elevated highways straight out of Usborne’s 1979 *The World of the Future* series by Kenneth Gatland and David Jefferis.

But what I wanted to write about was the **vision and strategy** that emanates from this city like all the rays in Laser Floyd compressed into a fluent 360 panorama of foresight. Yes, it was possible to build this extravagant futurity into existence because the central government can “**make it so**” but you have to know what to **make so**, and that starts with a vision.

We went to the [Shanghai Urban Planning Exhibition Center](https://www.travelchinaguide.com/attraction/shanghai/urban-planning-exhibtion-hall.htm), which tells a 6 storey story of the city in an exhibition called *Shanghai: Accelerating the Building of a Modern, Socialist, and International Metropolis with Global Influence.* They got me at “Better City, Better Life”. The show focuses on the interconnected vectors of the city and region.

One of these vectors is culture. From the exhibition text: **”Cultural centres are important spatial carriers of urban civilization”.** There’s just this vibe that shocked me with its simplicity: to have a good city you need all the things \- hospitals, schools, a place for elders, food, housing \- and one of those things is culture and cultural spaces.

By 2035 Shanghai aims to have “artistic venues and exhibition platforms” with “no fewer than 1\.5 museums, 4 libraries, 2\.5 performance venues, and 6 art galleries or museums” per 100,000 people. Now you may notice that museums are in here twice, so it’s not totally clear how to parse this, but I’m going to go with the conservative estimate and say they meant 1\.5 museums (including art museums) per 100,000 people (and the other part was about art galleries).

Metro Vancouver (2\.6 million people) has about 22 museums and municipal art museums, below the 40 we’d have with the benchmark China is aiming for. Canada (about 40 million people) has about 2500 museums. If [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_museums_in_British_Columbia) is correct, the province of BC has 258, which actually surprised me as a high number, although many of their collections are mainly comprised of hand\-made hands to replace those that were ripped off by threshers in 1899, bald velvet dolls, or blast caps from [Molybdenum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molybdenum) mines. (Don’t get me wrong I absolutely love small museums, the wierder the better).

They are also very large and free, and they’ve transitioned a huge previously industrial zone in [Shanghai West Bund district](https://www.westbund.com/en/index/ABOUT-WEST-BUND/Planning-Philosophy/Planning-Philosophy.html) into a cultural area almost as large as Central Park with crazy marvels like [Tank Shanghai](http://tankshanghai.com/?utm_source=ig&utm_medium=social&utm_content=link_in_bio&fbclid=PAZXh0bgNhZW0CMTEAc3J0YwZhcHBfaWQMMjU2MjgxMDQwNTU4AAGn45hJyvGF21Jf4CB04DVJ2Jv46TVzAwwFap258lzn1seUHpil2WcnLW51R7c_aem_GCKC8CwsNrG_vSJrj8hKxA), which is built in 5 huge containers previously used to store airplane fuel, [West Bund Museums](https://wbmshanghai.com/en) which is actually 3 venues, and a grab bag of others, and they’ve put it all alongside the AI and technology companies like Alibaba.

Shanghai demonstrates how cultural infrastructure, urban regeneration, and advanced tech ecosystems can be integrated into a single art\-led civic vision.
