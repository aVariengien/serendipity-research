Canada’s *[Pan\-Canadian AI Strategy](https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/ai-strategy/en)* wants to position the country as a global leader in artificial intelligence. It invests in research, talent, infrastructure, and governance. It builds world\-class institutes and funds cutting\-edge science. This is a serious forward\-looking plan but it is also **SRSLY** incomplete because it imagines AI as a primarily scientific and economic domain that speaks to industry, academia and regulators but fails to address culture, artists, or the creative ecosystems that help societies make sense of technological change.

##### The cultural sector in Canada

This omission matters because the future of AI will be shaped as much by meaning as by math and right now Canada is not preparing for that dimension of the story in any way.

In other places, cultural and creative sectors are being woven directly into the fabric of innovation policy. The result is a richer, more imaginative ecosystem that treats artists as co\-designers of the future. Ok so how?

## 1\. Embed Artists in Research and Development

I’ve already outlined some of the things going on in Europe that could provide direction for this country in terms of the role of creativity in R \& D. But to put it another way:

**Canada’s gap:** The Pan\-Canadian AI Strategy invests in institutes like [Mila](https://mila.quebec/en), [Vector](https://vectorinstitute.ai/), and [Amii](https://www.amii.ca/), but none of them systematically integrate artists into their research environments. There is no program equivalent to [STARTS](https://starts.eu/), no [national prize](https://starts.eu/what-we-do/prize/), and no expectation that artistic practice might shape technical R\&D.

**What we could do:** Launch a Canadian **Art \+ AI Program** modeled on [STARTS](https://starts.eu/), placing artists inside research labs, universities, and innovation clusters. Create a national prize recognizing transformative art–tech collaborations. Make artistic experimentation an explicit outcome of publicly funded AI research.

## 2\. Build Cultural Data Infrastructure

AI is built on data, and cultural data (everything from archives and collections to oral histories and craft practices) is one of the most valuable resources we have. But Canada has no national strategy for managing, structuring, or governing that data. There are no standards for consent or attribution, no shared infrastructure, and no mechanisms to make cultural materials machine\-readable or ethically usable in AI systems.

**Canada’s gap:** Our cultural datasets are fragmented, underfunded, and often inaccessible. They are rarely linked to AI research or policy. The Pan\-Canadian AI Strategy does not define cultural data, address its governance, or include cultural institutions as infrastructure partners.

**What we could do:** Establish a **Cultural Data Commons** — a national platform for structured, ethically governed cultural data. It should embed Indigenous data sovereignty and community consent, include public archives and collections, and be linked to public compute resources. Without this foundation Canadian culture is invisible to AI systems. [Here is Ana Serrano’s plea for a Cultural Data Commons](https://culturalpolicyhub.ocadu.ca/news/blog-building-ai-commons-canadas-future) published last week.

## 3\. Create New Kinds of Institutions

Europe is also reimagining the institutions that support cultural innovation. I’d mentioned a few. These institutions are not exhibitions or events — they are infrastructures designed for collaboration, experimentation, and public engagement over decades.

**Canada’s gap:** Our cultural infrastructure is still structured around 20th\-century models — museums, galleries, and granting programs organized by discipline. We lack dedicated spaces for cross\-sector collaboration and sustained experimentation with AI and emerging media.

**What we could do:** Establish **AI Culture Labs** across the country — permanent, interdisciplinary spaces co\-governed by cultural organizations, Indigenous communities, AI research centres. These labs could host residencies, fellowships, exhibitions, and public programs, positioning Canada as a leader in cultural AI experimentation.

## 4\. Rethink Curatorial Practice

At the 2023 **Helsinki Biennial**, curators experimented with [AI as a co\-curator](https://ocula.com/magazine/art-news/forward-looking-helsinki-biennial-engages-ai/) that explored the Helsinki Art Museum’s collections “to bring attention to what is not immediately visible to human perception, and to make new versions of the biennial”. [I’d also written about the](https://aifutures.substack.com/p/messages-from-the-messages?utm_source=publication-search) **[Venice Architecture Biennale](https://aifutures.substack.com/p/messages-from-the-messages?utm_source=publication-search)** project called [Spatial Intelligen](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/05/02/arts/design/sub-venice-architecture-biennale.html)s that uses a learning model trained on hundreds of photos of the exhibition and “answer\[s] visitors’ questions about the content of the show”, offering suggestions about how to navigate depending on interest. These are 2 cool projects that give us a glimpse of how AI could change curation, but they are also examples of the kinds of methodologies being explored and enacted through a cultural lens that aren’t being captured in our strategy.

**Canada’s gap:** Most Canadian museums and galleries are not resourced or incentivized to experiment with AI in curatorial (or any) contexts.

**What we could do:** Expand federal AI funding programs to include museums, galleries, and libraries. Create targeted grants for curatorial experimentation with AI, and support partnerships between cultural institutions and AI research centres.

## 5\. Expand the Definition of Culture

European programs explicitly include a broad range of creative practices, from sound art and fashion to game design and architecture. There just seems to be more space in these visions for artists working in domains that defy traditional categories \- and we are really going to be needing that kind of space.

**Canada’s gap:** Our cultural policy tends to put boxes inside the boxes, forgetting that ambiguous terrain is precisely where new AI\-enabled forms are emerging.

**What we could do:** Expand the definition of “culture” in federal frameworks to include all creative practices — visual and media arts, design, immersive and spatial practices, performance, sound, craft, game design, and beyond. Policy must anticipate the new categories that generative AI will create rather than clinging to old ones.

## 6\. Write Culture Into Funding and Policy

One of Europe’s most powerful tools is simple but effective: it writes culture into funding frameworks. Programs like **Horizon Europe** and **Creative Europe** require projects to address cultural outcomes and include cultural partners. Cultural diversity, discoverability, and benefit\-sharing are built into evaluation criteria.

**Canada’s gap:** Our AI funding programs — from research grants to compute access — never require cultural\-sector involvement or measure creative outcomes. You know how they say you can never say never? Here, we can say never.

**What we could do:** Integrate cultural KPIs and partnership requirements into federal AI programs. Require that a percentage of funded projects involve cultural organizations, contribute to cultural infrastructure, or address cultural questions.

## 7\. Build Benefit\-Sharing Mechanisms

Europe is beginning to explore benefit\-sharing models that compensate creators and communities when their cultural data trains AI systems. Some of this came out as suggestions from the [MONDIACULT 25 gathering and report](https://mondiacult2025.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/CULTAI_Report-of-the-Independent-Expert-Group-on-Artificial-Intelligence-and-Culture.pdf?_kx=RGE--e9XEqOkmkSaq8ZfJAiSNDaUFuad7U-RTAXt69o.UsW6sM). These include collective licensing, attribution frameworks, and levies on large AI models.

**Canada’s gap:** The Pan\-Canadian AI Strategy is silent on benefit\-sharing. While copyright reform is under discussion, there is no national framework for compensation tied to cultural data.

**What we could do:** Develop benefit\-sharing mechanisms and attribution tools for public AI systems. These could include cultural data trusts, collective licensing models, and revenue\-sharing schemes that ensure artists and communities benefit from the use of their cultural data.

## 8\. Position Cultural Institutions as AI Literacy Hubs

In Europe, museums and libraries are increasingly treated as frontline sites for AI literacy, public engagement, and creative experimentation. They host workshops, residencies, and public programs that demystify AI and invite communities into the conversation.

**Canada’s gap:** AI literacy is framed primarily as a workforce development issue, not a cultural one. GLAM institutions (**G**alleries, **L**ibraries, **A**rchives, **M**useums) are not integrated into the national AI strategy.

**What we could do:** Fund museums, libraries, and cultural centres to run AI\-literacy programs, artist residencies, and public dialogues. Position cultural institutions as critical civic spaces for understanding and shaping AI.

## 9\. Link Cultural AI to Sustainability

European cultural initiatives are increasingly linking AI experimentation to environmental responsibility — from carbon auditing of training processes to research on “frugal AI” design.

**Canada’s gap:** Environmental considerations are not built into cultural AI projects or funding streams.

**What we could do:** Require sustainability plans and energy\-efficient practices for cultural AI projects. Fund research into low\-carbon AI tools and infrastructure for the arts. It’s a perfect match for the experimentation artists are already leading with small datasets and small to medium sized models. (Shoutout to [MUTEK ecologies lab](https://clotmag.com/oped/toward-an-ecological-ai-introducing-muteks-ai-ecologies-lab-part-1) for one example).

## 10\. Embed Indigenous Protocols and Knowledge Governance

Canada has a unique opportunity and obligation to lead globally on Indigenous data sovereignty in the AI era. While the AI strategy references responsible AI broadly, it does not operationalize Indigenous governance principles in cultural data policy.

**What we could do:** Co\-develop data governance frameworks with Indigenous nations that embed OCAP principles and Indigenous approaches to knowledge stewardship. These frameworks should apply not only to Indigenous cultural data but also inform broader cultural data governance in Canada.

Sooooo \- the question is not whether we have the capacity because we totally do. It’s just a question of whether this time we’re going to treat culture as a strategic resource, or whether we want a one\- way ticket to Palookaville!
