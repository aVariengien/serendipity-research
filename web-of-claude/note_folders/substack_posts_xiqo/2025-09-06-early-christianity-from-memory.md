# early christianity (from memory)

*From the Greeks and Jews to the late Roman Empire*

Published: 2025-09-06
Source: https://xiqo.substack.com/p/early-christianity-from-memory

---

I write a snappy summary of this book Dominion - which gives a nice narrative gloss over all of Christianity - without consulting the book again. However, I’m happy with it because it gives you a deeper than usual narrative thread over thousands of years that you otherwise might not have.

The Greek pantheon didn’t have a consistent theology. People worshipped these archetypes. Paid attention to them, valued them, cultivated them, made sacrifices as credible signals of caring about them.

As I picture it, you worship warlike qualities and get warlike perks. Worship wisdom and learning, and you get the perks of knowledge. Worship blacksmithing or hunting, and etc. These Gods had competing wants and you just kind of balanced them. Sometimes your city “mained” a God, sometimes your family mained a God, etc.

Far away in Persia, their God Ahura Mazda was the first all good and all wise god, whose myth placed it in an eternal battle with darkness, or “the lie”.

In Roman society, strength was the most admirable quality. You were owed dignity to the degree that you were strong. It was fine to publicly crucify criminals and mistreat slaves. Also they cared about stoicism and thinking about stuff.

The Jewish god probably started as a tribal god, kind of like Jupiter, but evolved into an all powerful, all just god.

A personal reflection (mine): Worshipping an all powerful god is kind of the same as worshipping reality. I got the sense the author was arguing this led Jews to be more learning oriented, or have better epistemics, and therefore be better adapted the world. Since reality was for sure made by a good being, then it was “okay” to trust it and learn about it and update on failed predictions, and conform to it instead of fighting it.

Moses’ covenant

There was also a cool innovation - Jews had a covenant with God! Follow these rules -> be saved. None of the other cultures had an explicit deal with their Gods but the Jews did. They were special. They really cared about their special status - enough to resist attempts at conversion by Romans who conquered them and were mean to them for a long time. Also conversion to Judaism was hard - especially circumcision - so it didn’t spread very widely.

Then Jesus’ life happens. He just passed everyone’s vibe check so hard that some people started believing he was God in some deep sense. “The word become flesh.” (from John’s gospel).

Jesus casually passing the vibe check

My interpretation of this is that he was acting so purely from a place of loving cooperation that it was indistinguishable from a platonic ideal of deep caring and love and identification with others. This principle that if followed leads to attunement and deeper cooperation and at the limit to the kingdom of God.

Jesus’ God was for everyone instead of just for the tribe. He says things like “go and make disciples of all nations” (Matthew 28). He focused on the weakest and wretched and this emphasized that everyone is worth caring about, and looks like a precursor of caring about universal human rights.

What nations apostles went to

Jesus critiqued legalism and emphasized spirit over the letter of the law. Paul later said that the law of god can be read directly on the human heart. i.e. you should trust your intuition when making tough decisions. The Jews were very legalistic.

Saint Paul was a hater until he met Christ in a vision. Paul’s vision was so convincing that he dedicated his life to scaling the Church! He spent his life nomading and founding churches wherever he went. Then he would correspond with the churches he left behind to guide them, wrangling a bunch of Mediterranean cities with letters from afar.

Map of Paul's 3rd missionary journey

As an educated Jew, Paul made the crucial contribution of alloying Jewish morality and Greek philosophy. He grounded the bit about “reading morality from the human heart” in philosophy by connecting it with the Stoic concept of “conscience”.

As an example of “reading the law of god directly from the human heart”, in Galatia they were used to the rituals of their old (literally) castrating Goddess Cybelle, so he told them to chill with the circumcision. In Corinth, they were depraved and hedonistic so he told them to get a grip.

I thought Paul’s story was admirable. A philosopher and a builder like I aspire to be. Tragically died from Roman persecution, executed along with St. Peter, so you know he really cared.

Around 180 CE, Irenaeus was a bishop in Lyon who defended orthodoxy from gnostic heresies that were showing up at the time.

Gnostics claimed secret knowledge and Irenaeus fought them by defending apostolic succession and the rule of faith.

1. Apostolic succession means that your church must be in direct vibecheck lineage from the apostles - like “the greatest and most ancient church, founded and organized by the two most glorious apostles, Peter and Paul.” And the gnostics weren’t.
2. “Rule of faith” was a summary of what all the apostolic churches agreed on: 1) there is one creator god; 2) Jesus Christ existed, was crucified, rose, and was coming again; 3) the holy spirit spoke through prophets.

These defined an orthodoxy separate from gnostic “fantasy”. With this, belief itself became the defining marker of Christian identity. No need for ethnicity or law; you just needed to confess the faith. This was a massive deal - “first citizenship of belief alone”.

Origen, a 3rd century Alexandrian Greek and Jewish scholar in a lineage of teachers from Aristotle, was basically the father of Christian theology.

Christians kept being persecuted in the Roman Empire. Hardcore OG christians were often willing to die for their beliefs without renouncing Jesus. This was a big part of how Christianity had made it that far. They just really impressed everyone with their nerve.

Perpetua, early Christian martyr.

Some Christians weren’t as strong - like Caecilian who was a bishop in Carthage who gave up scriptures in order to survive persecution. Donatus, who was a bishop in North Africa, thought that was bullshit and condemned him. He didn’t think clergy who betrayed under persecution should validly administer sacraments. This became a big schism in the church. Eventually, after a bit of terrorism from both sides, this debate between “purity” and “compromise with the world” was resolved in favor of compromise and the losers were persecuted by Roman authorities.

Constantine converted because Christ won him a battle for the empire

Some time later still, emperor Constantine is trying to convert the empire to Christian (big day!) and calls the Council of Nicaea because nobody can agree whether Jesus was 1) of divine substance (same as the father); 2) merely similar; or 3) created. Divine substance wins and the losers are persecuted by Roman authorities.

Basil and two guys named Gregory were elite Romans form Capadoccia (bishops and theologians) in the 4th century and advocated (and practiced) radical charity, defended dignity for all, denounced slavery and the abandonment of babies (which Romans were apparently okay with).

Anthony and Pachomius were called the desert fathers and they wanted to radically imitate Christ. Anthony was a loner ascetic, a living saint. *Life of Anthony* (by Athanasius) went viral and helped make asceticism glamorous.

Pachomius went for community instead. He built the first cenobitic monasteries, where monks lived very strictly in hard labor, prayer, and obedience to an abbot. Whole federations of monasteries sprouted under this system.

Romans admired physical strength, but Christians were into SPIRITUAL strength. I.e. making up challenges to prove yourself. Hunger, celibacy, and battling your demons in the desert instead of enemies. This withdrawal from the world made them surprisingly influential since monasteries preserved literacy.

St. Martin of Tours carried monasticism to Gaul and was the first monk to become a bishop in the West. One of the last (poor and weak) became one of the first (rich and powerful)! Martin gave away all his possessions and viciously shamed the rich, but if the rich are bad, and we have no rich, where would charity come from???

He is famous in Portugal for in a snowy night offering his cape to a beggar who then revealed himself to be Jesus.

As a final reflection, I was struck by how Christianity was a heavy mix of Greek and Jewish culture. I was also impressed by Paul as an icon, and by the prominence of Gaul (France) in early Christianity, given that it started all the way in the Middle East.
