# medieval christendom (from memory)

*Christianity moves its center of gravity from the eastern Mediterranean to Western Europe and evolves into its “Christendom” form.*

Published: 2025-10-08
Source: https://xiqo.substack.com/p/medieval-christendom-from-memory

---

This is my favorite period of the story. Lots of geopolitics and epic power shifts. Christianity moves its center of gravity from the Eastern Mediterranean to Western Europe and glows up into a realm in its own right - *Christendom*.

In this episode

* Monks are rock stars (c. 400–800)
* Islam pushes Christians from the East to Europe (630s–750s)
* The Franks become the main character of Christianity (714–888)
* Everybody wants to be Christian (800s-1000s)
* The Pope owns the emperor (1050–1100s)

**Monks are rock stars**

[Remember the desert fathers Anthony and Pachomius?](https://xiqo.substack.com/p/early-christianity-from-memory) Anthony was a loner ascetic, a living saint. Pachomius was a monk and founded monasteries. They wanted to radically imitate Christ. Christians were really into this.

In this period, kings relied on holiness for legitimacy. The most obviously source of “holy” were chad ascetics and monks who lived like Christ, like the Anthony and Pachomius. The mandate of the desert fathers was picked up at the other edge of the world in Ireland.

Saint Patrick was a 5th c. Roman Briton taken to Ireland as a slave. He lived a life of discipline, missionary work, and church-building. He thought leaving your family and roots was the fastest way to depend solely on God, and as a result was seen as super holy and hardcore.

Columbanus was an Irish monk in the 6th c. in the same tradition so he left home for Francia and established monasteries in Luxueil and Bobbiòwhile establishing a cult following from his discipline and asceticism, seeking out the wilderness to survive and build in.

Ascetics and monks aside, this is largely the story of how after the Muslim conquests, Franco-Germanic Europe’s goes from being pagan to being the primary vessel of Christianity.

### **A new vessel for Christianity**

***Islam pushes Christians from the East to Europe***

Islam rises in the 600s. A new people of the book. Mohammad restores Abrahamic-style monotheism. Muslims go back to a legalistic interpretation of the word of god like the Jews (sharia), rather than law written in the hearts of men like in Christianity. Arab conquests of the Sasanian Empire and Syria 620–630

The Arab conquests go CRAZY. The Arabs come up as Rome and Persia are finishing a devastating meat-grinder war (602–628). They go from a small tribe to conquering the Arabian Peninsula to conquering the Entire Persian Empire and taking massive chunks of Eastern Rome and North Africa like Syria, Palestine, Egypt, Libya.

Arab cavalry pursue fleeing Byzantines

Carthage, a bastion of Christianity, is conquered by 698. This sends African refugees to Europe. Theodore of Tarsus and Hadrian the African find refuge in England, found a top-tier school in Canterbury, and reorganize the English church. Saint Bede, an Anglo-Saxon monk in their tradition, gave England a story to aim towards: one Christian people, a new Jerusalem. Oh he also popularised time as BC or AD.

There’s something about both France and England believing themselves to have sacred destinies and they do play very important roles over Christian history, *especially* France.

**The Franks become the main character of Christianity**

The great original wellsprings of Christianity: Syria, Palestine, Egypt, Africa are inaccessible, the Mediterranean was (militarily) a Saracen sea, the world cut in half.

By 732 the Muslims had conquered most of Spain and were raiding into France. At Poitiers in modern France, Charles Martel turns back the Muslims and his descendants conquer everything from the Pyrenees to the Danube. As a result, the Carolingians (Franks) became the very character of Christian rule.

Charlemagne conquers the Saxons through brutal genocide of Roman proportions - annual crop burnings, destruction, massive killings, forced conversions, cutting down ancient sacred oak trees. In 800, Charlemagne is at the head of the first power of Roman proportions in the West. So he is crowned Augustus by the pope!

*The Destruction of Irminsul by Charlemagne* by [Heinrich Leutemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Leutemann) (1882).

For centuries this crowning will be a source of drama about who appoints who. If Peter crowns Caesar, Peter can un-crown; if Caesar guards Peter, he will appoint Peter’s men.

Charlemagne had a stellar advisor named Alcuin. Alcuin was from the British tradition of Theodore and Hadrian and he who convinced the big guy to ease up on forced conversions because he thought faith needs to come from one’s own will, through gentle persuasion at most.

Alcuin becomes abbot of Tours in 797 and makes the monastery compile, copy and distribute standardized bibles in the latin vulgate. This brought Christ deeper and deeper into the Frankish countryside, with all aspects of life touched. They also copied handbooks for priests, who stood as the ultimate sources of knowledge for their flocks. Parishes and nuclear families started weakening the tribal kinship ties, and the social fabric of Europe starts looking very different.

In the few centuries that follow Christianity faces perils and NEW HEIGHTS of GLORY.

### **Conversion**

**Everyone wants to be Christian**

For a century christendom looked fragile - divided kingdoms, constant raids. In the 840s, Charlemagne dies and his empire is divided into 3: basically the territories corresponding to modern France, Italy, and Germany. Shortly after, in 846, the Arabs sack Rome, and the Vikings went around overthrowing British and Irish kingdoms. Uh oh.

In 955 Hungarians (horse nomads from the Central Asian steppe) raid and try to conquer Bavaria. Otto, a descendant of Saxons (NOT Frankish) is elected to rule the East Franks. Otto repels the Hungarians and is crowned emperor in Rome!

Aethelstan’s victory at the battle of Brunanburh

Broadly, Christian forces everywhere start recovering. Aethelstan king of Wessex beats back the great Viking invasion in 937, unifying Britain (and fulfilling Bede’s vision of a united realm in England).

In this period we see battle as the ultimate testing ground of a God’s authority. If you win, your god is clearly better.[2](https://xiqo.substack.com/p/medieval-christendom-from-memory#footnote-2-174146469)

Since the Christians are winning so much, Pagans start considering joining the family. Hungarian king Stephen converts 50 years after the big Hungarian defeat. By 1038, the Bohemians, Poles, Danes, Norwegians all convert.

For all this glory, there was the paradox of Christ’s suffering. Around this time, Christ goes from being depicted as alive and victorious to being represented dead or suffering on the cross. This shift in ethos resonates with what we’re about to see: a reversal in dominance between material and spiritual power.

### **Reform**

**The Pope owns the emperor**

Towards the end of the 11th century, priests had been contaminated by secular powers for a long time. Pope Gregory VII comes to power in 1073 and is having none of that so he starts reining in the clergy. Namely, he was fighting:

* Simony: buying church offices;
* Nicolaitism: priest marriage;
* Lay investiture: secular rulers appointing bishops.

This is annoying to nobles who liked appointing bishops. It’s ESPECIALLY annoying to German emperor Henry IV, who is so mad he tries to depose the pope. HOWEVER Gregory says “no u” and Deposes The Emperor Instead. Gregory does it by excommunicating Henry from the church, which gives all his rivals a Schelling opportunity to go - “hey guys Henry is a Bad Person let’s take his stuff” - and press him.

[![undefined](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!QOfz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2aa5e6f3-edd4-4216-af37-5de83cb40626_517x338.jpeg "undefined")](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!QOfz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2aa5e6f3-edd4-4216-af37-5de83cb40626_517x338.jpeg)

Things got so bad that the emperor humiliated himself by showing up barefoot at the pope’s winter home at Canossa and begging forgiveness. The Pope forgives him. Henry restarts the war right away.

Canossa marked a new reality: popes could unmake kings by excommunicating them, turning their vassals against them. The church had found a weapon against secular force.

Things are going so well that the next Pope calls the first Crusade. It shouldn’t have worked: it was a ragtag army, facing insane logistics problems, with no unified command.

After the successful siege of Jerusalem in 1099, Godfrey of Bouillon, leader of the First Crusade, became the first ruler of the Kingdom of Jerusalem.

Mind bogglingly, it succeeds in 1099 thanks to Byzantine support, divided Seljuk enemy, and immense luck. They conquered the Holy Land.

What happened was the cementing of *Christendom* as a realm. With the Pope’s authority over kings confirmed by a reconquered Holy Land, the Church becomes a networked jurisdiction - a Christian empire that willed itself into being not by force of arms but by the force of belief!

Organizing your belief systems around nature must be more adaptive when nature is a much bigger force than you and you’re still at its mercy in hunter-gatherer or herding or early farming mode. As humans and human society and culture become dominant forces, it make sense that the emphasis shifts away from nature to something else - in this case something like the kingdom of God from treating your human neighbors compassionately, rather than, say, communing with the spirit of the grove or the spring. I’ve been curious about the mythological roots of my home region of Portugal and Spain and dipping into on of the very few books that details shards of myths with roots in the neolithic, which were still present in the 20th century, although syncretized with Christianity. Often the central characters in these myths would be “Mouras encantadas”, and they seem to be something like the spirits of the places. It made me wonder if there’s a case for RETVRN or if it’s actually good that we emphasize other things now given that springs and woods and landmark boulders are barely present in our lives at all anymore. I think there’s something to be said about these places being ecologically richer, having especially dense Feng Shui energy in the landscape and therefore possessing special distributed intelligence in the sense that Michael Levin talks about. In a pre-bicameral split society, they might induce visions, and even now they might induce inspiration at an embodied level.
